Accessibility Guide

Why Accessibility Matters

Accessible web design ensures that every student—regardless of ability or disability—can access the information and services they need. Roughly one in five adults live with some form of disability, so an inaccessible website can block a significant portion of your audience[1]. Institutions of higher education are often legally required to meet accessibility requirements and should proactively implement inclusive practices rather than wait for legal challenges[1].

Design Principles

Provide Sufficient Contrast

Text needs to stand out clearly from its background so that people with low vision or colour‑blindness can read it. Ensure there is enough contrast between foreground and background colours for paragraph text, icons, buttons and images[2].

Use More Than Colour to Communicate

Colour should not be the only way you convey meaning. For example, required form fields shouldn’t be indicated solely in red; include symbols or labels as well[2].

Identify Interactive Elements

Links and buttons should have distinct styles and visible focus states for keyboard navigation. Users need to know when they are interacting with an element and be able to reach everything using the keyboard[2].

Clear Navigation

Navigation menus should be consistent in naming, styling and positioning. Provide more than one way to locate content (for example, search, site map or breadcrumbs)[2].

Labels for Form Elements

Every form field must have a descriptive label adjacent to the input box. Labels are generally placed to the left or above the field, ensuring a clear association[2].

Provide Feedback

Forms and interactive components should provide clear feedback when something goes wrong—show error messages in a prominent way and explain how to fix them[2].

Organise Content With Headings and Spacing

Use headings, lists and whitespace to group related content. Clear structure helps users with cognitive or reading difficulties follow the information[2].

Design for Different Screen Sizes

When viewed on a mobile phone or with large text, layouts should adapt to a single column and keep navigation accessible. The presentation of key elements such as headers and navigation should adjust to make the best use of space[3].

Include Alternatives for Media

Provide alternatives such as transcripts for audio, captions for videos and text descriptions for complex graphics. Collaborate with content authors and developers to supply these alternatives[3].

Provide Controls for Auto‑Playing Content

Any automatically playing audio, video or carousel should include controls to pause, stop or hide the content. This prevents users from being distracted or triggered by unexpected movement[3].

The Four Accessibility Principles

  1. Perceivable: Provide text alternatives for non‑text content (e.g. images and emoticons), supply captions for media and ensure content can be presented in various ways while preserving structure[4].
  2. Operable: Make sure all functionality can be accessed via keyboard, give users enough time to read and use content, avoid triggers for seizures and help users find their location[4].
  3. Understandable: Use clear language, make pages behave predictably and help users avoid and correct mistakes[4].
  4. Robust: Build websites with valid, semantic markup so they can work with a range of current and future assistive technologies[1].
